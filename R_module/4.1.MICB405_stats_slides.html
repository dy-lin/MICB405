Specimen vs. Sample vs. Population

  • Specimens A1, A2, A3, etc. taken from
  • Sample A, which is taken from
  • Population A




  • Common language "sample" for 1 biological replicate = Specimen
  • \(\neq\) Statistical meaning of "sample"

Statistic vs. Parameter

  • Use a sample to calculate statistics
  • Statistics estimate parameters of the overall population
  • In real world systems, we can never truly measure a parameter
  • Statistic: Mean weight of 10 beavers (sample) measured in BC
  • Parameter: Mean weight of beavers in BC (population) including all individuals residing here

Using statistics

  • Use statistics of samples to draw conclusions about underlying populations
  • Compare statistics of samples from different populations in statistical tests
  • t-test, ANOVA, linear model, etc.

t-test

  • Compares 2 sample means
  • Null hypothesis H0: the two population means are equal
  • Alternate hypothesis H1: the two population means are not equal


  • Hypotheses are about the underlying populations even though we only have data from samples
  • The alternate says nothing about greater than / less than

t-test assumptions

  1. Simple random sample
  2. Approximately normal distributions of sample means
  3. Reasonably large sample size
  4. Equal sample sizes*
  5. Equal sample variances*

\(*\) Different equations exist if these are not true

t-test equation

Assumes unequal variance

\[t = \frac{\bar x_1 -\bar x_2}{\sqrt(\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2})}\]

  • numerator = difference in sample means
  • denominator = measure of sample variance in standard deviation (s) relative to the sample size (n)

The p-value

  • Probability value
  • Measure of significance


  • Probability that the difference in means would be \(\geq\) the observed results if the true population means are not different (H0)
  • Probabilty of a false positive
  • Probability that you conclude that the population means are different when they're actually not

Levels of significance

p < 0.05

  • Common across multiple fields
  • Up to 5% probability of a false positive


  • Are you comfortable with p < 0.05 to test if a probiotic is associated with:
  • gut microbiota composition?
  • incidence of gastrointestinal disease?
  • a serious adverse side effect?

Defining the level of significance

  • Based on research question and data types
  • Must be done prior to running statistical tests


  • Avoid p hacking!
  • Don't change the p cutoff to fit your observed results
  • Don't repeat or alter statistical tests with the goal of obtaining a significant result

What if p = 0?

  • Results due to rounding error
  • Does not mean you are 100% confident that the population means are truly different
  • Should be reported as p < the minimum value that the program will report, like p < 2E-16 for R
